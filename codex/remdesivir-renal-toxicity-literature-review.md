# Renal Toxicity of Remdesivir: Short Literature Review

## Question

Does remdesivir cause clinically important renal toxicity, and how should the current evidence be interpreted in patients with reduced kidney function?

## Bottom line

The current literature does not support remdesivir as a major intrinsic nephrotoxin during standard short-course treatment for COVID-19. Early concern was driven by three issues: exclusion of patients with severe renal impairment from pivotal trials, the possibility of accumulation of the solubilizing excipient sulfobutylether-beta-cyclodextrin (`SBECD`), and early pharmacovigilance signals suggesting disproportionate reporting of acute renal failure. However, later comparative observational studies, a secondary randomized analysis, and the phase 3 `REDPINE` trial in patients with severe kidney dysfunction did not show a clear increase in kidney toxicity attributable to remdesivir itself.

The remaining uncertainty is not whether kidney events occur during remdesivir treatment, but whether those events are caused by the drug rather than by severe COVID-19, sepsis, hemodynamic instability, baseline chronic kidney disease (`CKD`), or acute kidney injury (`AKI`) already present before treatment. Across the better-controlled studies, remdesivir generally appears renal-safe enough for use in patients with impaired kidney function when clinically indicated.

## Why concern arose

Initial regulatory caution was biologically plausible. Remdesivir is formulated with `SBECD`, a renally cleared excipient that can accumulate when glomerular filtration is severely reduced. Earlier reviews noted that high animal exposures to `SBECD` were associated with liver and renal tubular injury, although these exposures were much higher than those used in short human treatment courses. In addition, some nucleoside and nucleotide analogs can cause proximal tubular injury after prolonged exposure, which made nephrotoxicity a reasonable early concern even though remdesivir is usually given for only 3 to 10 days.

The earliest human safety alarm came from pharmacovigilance rather than controlled clinical comparison. Gerard et al. analyzed World Health Organization `VigiBase` data and found a statistically significant disproportionality signal for acute renal failure with remdesivir relative to hydroxychloroquine, tocilizumab, and lopinavir/ritonavir. That study was useful as signal detection, but it could not establish causality because spontaneous reporting is vulnerable to confounding by indication, stimulated reporting, missing denominators, and the high background incidence of AKI in hospitalized COVID-19.

## Clinical evidence

Subsequent studies moved the field from signal detection toward causal inference.

Small early case series in patients with `AKI`, `CKD`, or dialysis dependence did not show a strong nephrotoxicity signal. For example, Thakare et al. reported short-course use in patients with acute or chronic kidney disease without major treatment-limiting renal safety concerns. Pettit et al. similarly concluded that use in severe renal impairment appeared relatively safe, while noting that more creatinine rises occurred in that subgroup but were not clearly attributable to remdesivir because most affected patients already had AKI before treatment.

More informative evidence came from comparator-based designs. Seethapathy et al. performed a propensity-matched retrospective cohort study in hospitalized patients with impaired kidney function and found no significant difference in adverse kidney outcomes between remdesivir-treated patients and untreated comparators, including peak creatinine, doubling of creatinine, kidney replacement therapy, and estimated glomerular filtration rate at day 90. A self-controlled case series from Hong Kong also found that the risk of AKI was not significantly higher during remdesivir exposure windows than during the pre-exposure period, which argues against a large acute nephrotoxic effect.

The strongest evidence in severe kidney dysfunction came later. In the `CATCO` randomized secondary analysis published on August 29, 2022, patients with baseline `eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m2` who received remdesivir had no increased risk of day-5 toxic kidney effects, new dialysis, or mortality relative to standard care; the authors concluded that remdesivir could be safely administered in kidney dysfunction, while acknowledging small numbers. The phase 3 `REDPINE` trial, published in 2024, directly enrolled hospitalized patients with `AKI`, `CKD`, or kidney failure including dialysis patients. Although underpowered for efficacy, `REDPINE` found no new safety signal and concluded that remdesivir was safe in severe kidney impairment, with pharmacokinetic data supporting no dose adjustment even when `eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m2`.

Real-world evidence since then has been broadly consistent. Yang et al. performed a propensity score matched analysis in patients with `eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m2` and found no aggravation in creatinine or `eGFR` by day 5, and remdesivir was not an independent risk factor for new dialysis. Some crude outcome differences reflected baseline illness severity rather than a specific toxic drug effect.

## Mechanistic interpretation

The available data suggest that the original renal concern was driven more by exposure uncertainty than by a clearly demonstrated clinical toxicity syndrome. The current FDA label notes that exposures of the remdesivir metabolites `GS-441524` and `GS-704277`, as well as `SBECD`, are increased in renal impairment, including in dialysis patients. Even so, the FDA-approved label now states that no dosage adjustment is recommended for any degree of renal impairment, including dialysis. The label further notes that safety results in the severe renal impairment randomized trial were consistent with those observed in other adult remdesivir trials.

This pattern supports a practical distinction:

- Pharmacokinetic accumulation does occur in severe renal impairment.
- Clinically important short-course nephrotoxicity has not been clearly demonstrated in controlled human studies.

That does not mean renal monitoring is unnecessary. It means that worsening kidney function during COVID-19 treatment should not automatically be attributed to remdesivir without considering the much stronger competing causes of AKI in this population.

## Synthesis

Taken together, the evidence has evolved from theoretical concern and pharmacovigilance signal generation toward relative clinical reassurance. The most defensible current conclusion is that remdesivir is not proven to be free of renal risk, but any direct nephrotoxic effect is likely small compared with the background renal risk of severe COVID-19 and critical illness. For short courses, especially 3- to 5-day regimens, the balance of evidence favors use when remdesivir is otherwise indicated, including in patients with severe renal impairment.

The main residual limitations of the literature are:

- many earlier studies were retrospective and vulnerable to confounding by severity;
- several cohorts were small and underpowered for rare toxic events;
- most data concern short-course treatment, so the conclusions should not be overextended to prolonged exposure.

## Conclusion

The center of gravity of the literature has shifted. Early signal-detection studies justified caution and monitoring, but later controlled human data do not support a strong remdesivir-specific renal toxicity signal. In 2026, the most evidence-based position is that remdesivir can be used in patients with renal impairment, including severe impairment and dialysis, when clinically indicated, with standard clinical monitoring and careful attribution of AKI to competing causes.
